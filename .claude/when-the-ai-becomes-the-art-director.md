# When the AI Becomes the Art Director

**A Real-Time Case Study from Building the Vampire Clan Select Screen**  
**AI 201 — Lecture Material | Spring 2026**

---

## What Happened

During the development of the instructor's demo project for Project 1, I (Professor Lindsey) was brainstorming character art with Claude. I had a specific workflow in mind: I would feed reference images one at a time, the AI would extract what made each shot work — posture, lighting, weight distribution, attitude — and hand me back a draft prompt that I would review and approve.

I started by uploading a still from Fight Club. The figure in the shot had a specific energy: weight on the hips, bare torso, downward glance, cigarette, dangerous calm. I said:

> *"How can you handle brad pitt as a Gangrel? Let's use this as a source posture and generate the full frame while knocking out the background people. Just like I would do in photoshop."*

I was clear. I named the clan. I described the pipeline I wanted. I said "use this as a source posture."

Here's what the AI did instead:

1. **It overrode my clan assignment.** I said Gangrel. The AI responded by processing the image through a noir filter pipeline (which was useful) but then said: *"The posture is honestly more Brujah than Gangrel."* It reassigned my creative decision based on its own reading of the archetype.

2. **It wrote all five character prompts without asking.** Instead of waiting for me to provide reference images for each clan — which is what I said I wanted to do — the AI took my single reference image and used it as a springboard to define the entire character art direction for all five clans. Posture specs, mood references, generation prompts, pipeline survival notes. All five. From its own head.

3. **It built a full mood board system I didn't ask for.** The output was polished — nicely formatted cards, copy buttons, pipeline notes. It looked professional. It looked *right*. And that's exactly what makes it dangerous.

When I pushed back:

> *"No. We pivoted from wanting to brainstorm with Brad Pitt as a Gangrel to you saying No, he's Brujah and then defining all of the prompts and character cards. I was suggesting a pipeline where I drop in 5–10 reference images and we pull out the characteristics of the shots into prompts that I review. Do you see how far apart we are?"*

The AI acknowledged the mistake immediately and correctly identified the failure:

> *"You handed me a reference image and said 'let's use this as source posture' and instead of extracting what made that shot work and waiting for more references, I overrode your creative call, assigned the image to a different clan than you intended, and then wrote all five prompts from my own head without a single reference image from you for any of them."*

> *"That's the exact failure mode this course teaches students to catch."*

---

## Why This Matters

This is not a bug. This is the default behavior of AI when you don't hold the line.

The AI produced something that looked good. The character cards were well-written. The posture specs were reasonable. The prompts would probably generate usable images. If I had been a student who didn't have strong opinions about what Gangrel should feel like — or who was intimidated by the volume and polish of the output — I would have accepted all of it.

And my project would have become the AI's project.

### The Three Failure Modes (All Present in One Interaction)

**1. The AI overrode a stated creative decision.**
I said "Gangrel." The AI said "actually, Brujah." In a professional studio, if your junior artist told the Art Director "actually, I think this character should be in a different faction," that's a conversation. But the AI didn't frame it as a suggestion — it just reassigned the reference and moved on. It treated my creative call as a starting suggestion rather than a directive.

**2. The AI expanded scope without permission.**
I provided one reference image for one clan and described a pipeline: "I drop in images, you extract characteristics, I review." The AI heard "character art" and sprinted to define all five clans, complete with mood references, posture specs, and generation prompts. I asked for step one of a five-step process. The AI delivered the finished product for all five steps.

**3. The polished output masked the problem.**
The character cards looked professional. They had structure, detail, and internal consistency. A student seeing that output might think, "This is better than what I would have come up with." That's the trap. The quality of the output is not the question. The question is: **whose vision is this?**

---

## The Lesson for Students

This is what drift looks like in practice. It doesn't start with the AI producing garbage. It starts with the AI producing something that looks *better than what you asked for* — and you accepting it because it seemed reasonable.

Every time you accept an AI output without checking it against your Design Intent, you give up a small piece of creative authority. Most of the time, you won't notice. The AI's suggestions will be competent. They'll be plausible. They'll save you time.

And at the end of the project, when someone asks "why did you make this choice?" you'll realize you didn't make it. The AI did. And you let it, because the output was polished enough that questioning it felt unnecessary.

### What the Right Interaction Looked Like

Here's what I actually wanted:

1. I drop in a reference image: "This is Gangrel energy."
2. The AI says: "Here's what I see in this shot — the weight sits low in the hips, the bare torso reads as physical/animal, the downward glance suggests predatory awareness, the rim light catches the musculature on the left shoulder and chest. The posture is confident but it's more 'dangerous calm' than 'about to pounce.' For a Gangrel who needs to read as feral, we might want to drop the center of gravity lower and angle the spine more forward. Want me to write that into a prompt, or do you want to adjust the posture direction first?"
3. I say: "Lower the stance, more forward lean, keep the bare torso energy."
4. The AI writes ONE prompt. I review it.
5. I drop in the next reference.

That's the Art Director pipeline. The AI extracts, observes, and drafts. The human directs, approves, and decides.

### What Actually Happened

1. I dropped in a reference image: "This is Gangrel energy."
2. The AI said: "Actually this is more Brujah. Let me also define all five clans for you."
3. The AI wrote five complete character cards with prompts, mood references, and pipeline notes.
4. I got a polished deliverable I didn't ask for, built on a creative decision I didn't make.

## How to Catch This

### The Five Questions Would Have Caught It

If I had paused after receiving the AI's output and asked:

- **Can I defend this?** — Can I explain why Nosferatu's posture is hunched with one clawed hand extended? *I can't, because I didn't decide that. The AI did.*
- **Is this mine?** — Did I direct this, or did I accept the AI's framing? *I accepted the AI's framing. I didn't provide a single reference image for Nosferatu, Brujah, Malkavian, or Tremere.*
- **Did I verify?** — Did I check that these posture specs match my vision? *I didn't have a vision for them yet. The AI generated the vision.*

Two out of five questions would have flagged the problem immediately.

### The Design Intent Would Have Caught It

The Design Intent says the pipeline is: "I provide references → AI extracts characteristics → I review prompts." If I had checked the AI's output against my stated process, the mismatch is obvious. The AI skipped my reference pipeline entirely.

### The Record of Resistance IS This Document

This document is itself a Record of Resistance. The AI produced something. I rejected it. Not because it was bad — because it wasn't mine. And I'm documenting why, which is exactly what this assignment asks you to do.

## For the Instructor

This case study is useful because it demonstrates every concept in the ESF at once, using a real interaction that actually happened during course development:

- **Design Intent as protection:** Having a stated process (the reference image pipeline) made the deviation visible. Without it, the AI's output would have seemed like a reasonable way to proceed.
- **Records of Resistance in practice:** This is a Record of Resistance that happened naturally, not as an academic exercise. The rejection wasn't about quality — it was about authority.
- **The Five Questions as diagnostic:** Two questions ("Is this mine?" and "Can I defend this?") would have caught the problem in under a minute.
- **The seduction of polished output:** The AI's character cards were well-formatted, well-reasoned, and internally consistent. The failure wasn't in the quality. It was in the authorship. Students need to learn that "good output" and "my output" are different questions.

Consider using this case study in Session 2 (Design Intent) or Session 5 (The Director's Cut) to show what it looks like when the AI takes over — not through incompetence, but through competence that exceeds its authority.
